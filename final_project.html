In today’s hyper-connected world, lithium batteries power everything from smartphones and laptops to electric toothbrushes and portable chargers. As our reliance on these rechargeable batteries grows, so does the concern surrounding their safety—particularly when it comes to air travel. Over the past decade, lithium battery incidents on airplanes have sparked increasing scrutiny from airlines, aviation authorities, and security experts alike. Why Are Lithium Batteries a Risk in the Air? Lithium-ion batteries are compact and energy-dense, making them ideal for modern electronics. However, under certain conditions—like physical damage, manufacturing defects, or exposure to heat—they can short-circuit, overheat, and catch fire. In the controlled environment of a flight cabin, where the crew must manage hundreds of passengers in a pressurized metal tube 35,000 feet above the ground, such an event can be catastrophic. “Lithium batteries are meant to be put away before boarding flights,” says Justin Baum, head of security at Syracuse Hancock International Airport. “They can cause problems once in the air if they start to overheat.” When a lithium battery goes into what experts call “thermal runaway,” it can quickly reach temperatures above 1,000°F, leading to intense fires that are difficult to extinguish with water. What’s more alarming is that a small battery in a laptop or phone can ignite nearby items, potentially endangering the entire cabin. Documented Incidents and Growing Concerns The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) has recorded hundreds of lithium battery-related incidents since 2006. These include devices catching fire in overhead bins, under seats, or inside checked baggage. In 2023 alone, more than 60 such incidents were reported in the U.S., many involving power banks, vape pens, and e-bikes with large batteries. For example, in February 2023, a flight from San Diego to Newark had to make an emergency landing after a passenger's external battery charger caught fire mid-flight. Crew members responded quickly using a special fire containment bag, but several passengers were treated for smoke inhalation. Airlines have responded by tightening restrictions. Delta, American Airlines, and United all limit the size and type of lithium batteries allowed on board. Most follow the International Air Transport Association (IATA) guidelines, which cap personal lithium-ion batteries at 100 watt-hours (Wh). For reference, most smartphones have batteries between 5 and 15 Wh, while laptops range from 30 to 90 Wh. “The general limit for Watt-hours on planes internationally is 100,” Baum explains. “If over that, the risk is higher for problems. The reason they are carry-on items is so flight attendants can respond quickly.”

Carry-On vs. Checked Luggage One of the most important airline safety rules is that spare lithium batteries must be carried on board—not placed in checked luggage. The rationale is simple: if a fire starts in the cabin, flight attendants can respond immediately with fire extinguishers and thermal containment bags. In contrast, a fire in the cargo hold might go undetected until it’s too late. Airlines like Emirates and Lufthansa have specific protocols for battery-powered medical devices and mobility aids, while budget airlines like Ryanair and EasyJet require approval for any spare batteries above 100 Wh. Some carriers even mandate fireproof cases for transporting high-capacity batteries used in professional film equipment. In 2016, the FAA banned the Samsung Galaxy Note 7 from all flights after a series of battery explosions. The incident served as a wake-up call to the aviation industry about how even mainstream electronics can become hazardous. Evolving Regulations and Preventive Measures To prevent accidents, the FAA and international aviation authorities have developed detailed guidelines for the transport of lithium batteries. Passengers are advised to: Keep battery-powered devices in their carry-on bags. Turn off devices during takeoff and landing. Use only manufacturer-approved chargers. Avoid overcharging devices before a flight. Protect batteries from being crushed or punctured. Airports are also stepping up education efforts. Posters, announcements, and TSA checkpoint signs frequently remind travelers about battery safety. At some airports, like Syracuse Hancock International, security staff are trained to question passengers carrying large battery packs or unfamiliar devices. A Shared Responsibility While airline crews are equipped to handle emergencies, the primary responsibility falls on passengers to follow the rules. Many incidents could be prevented by proper packing and battery handling. “Passengers often underestimate how much energy is stored in these small devices,” says Baum. “They may seem harmless, but if something goes wrong at 35,000 feet, it can escalate very quickly.” Some forward-thinking airlines have begun testing new technologies, including fire-containment boxes in cargo holds and smart bins in cabins that can detect smoke. Meanwhile, aircraft manufacturers like Boeing and Airbus continue to collaborate with battery experts to enhance safety standards. The Future of Battery Safety in Aviation As battery technology evolves—especially with the rise of electric aircraft and drones—the aviation industry will continue to face new challenges. The key will be balancing innovation with safety. Education, regulation, and engineering must work hand-in-hand. With the right protocols in place and awareness among travelers, the risks associated with lithium batteries can be minimized, ensuring that the benefits of portable power don’t come at the cost of flight safety.
